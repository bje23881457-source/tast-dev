The first thing visitors notice about Gyeongju is that it has too many hills. Soft green mounds rise between the houses, along the roads, in the middle of parks. Then I tell them the truth: these are not hills. They are the sleeping kings and queens of Silla.

## A city built among its dead

The largest cluster is **Daereungwon**, where twenty-three mounds share a single walled park. Silla royalty were buried under enormous heaps of river stones, then sealed beneath layer upon layer of clay and earth until each tomb became a grassy hill. That construction had a quiet gift for us: because the stone piles collapsed inward, most tombs could never be robbed. What was placed inside stayed inside.

> The Silla did not hide their dead underground. They raised them up, into the shape of the land itself.

## The Heavenly Horse and its crown

The most famous of these is **Cheonmachong**, the Tomb of the Heavenly Horse. When it was excavated in the 1970s, archaeologists found a saddle flap painted with a white horse in mid-gallop, wings of speed streaming from its legs — the image that gave the tomb its name. Beside it lay a **golden crown**, delicate as breath, its uprights shaped like trees and antlers and hung with curved jade and gold spangles.

## Walking among them at night

On the night walk I save the mounds for last. Under low lighting they lose their edges and become pure silhouette, one dark curve behind another. Stand quietly and you feel the strange gentleness of it — a whole dynasty asleep beneath the grass, its gold long lifted into the light, its kings still holding the shape of these unhurried green hills.
