Guests often walk past Cheomseongdae without slowing down. From a distance it is only a curved tower of granite, about nine metres tall, sitting quietly in the grass. But I always ask them to stop and count with me, because this little bottle of stone has been reading the heavens for nearly fourteen centuries.

## Built by a queen who watched the stars

Cheomseongdae was raised in the 7th century, during the reign of Queen Seondeok — Silla's first ruling queen. In an agricultural kingdom, knowing the seasons was power: when to plant, when to harvest, when the rains would come. An observatory was not decoration. It was statecraft, and it was survival.

> To govern the land, Silla first learned to read the sky above it.

## The mathematics hidden in the stones

Look closely and the tower becomes a calendar. It is built from roughly **362 stones** — close to the number of days in a lunar year. They are laid in **27 circular layers**, honouring Seondeok as Silla's **27th monarch**. Twelve stones form the base, and twelve more the upper section — the months of the year — while a square window near the middle divides the body into twelve courses above and twelve below.

## Seeing it after dark

I bring guests here on the night walk, when the crowds have thinned and the granite turns silver under the moon. Stand at the base and look up through the window opening, imagining a Silla astronomer climbing inside to watch a comet or an eclipse. You are not looking at a monument to the past. You are standing beneath the same sky they measured — a kingdom's quiet attempt to hold the whole year in a single tower of stone.
