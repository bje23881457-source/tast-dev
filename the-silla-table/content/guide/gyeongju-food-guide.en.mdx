Gyeongju food is not flashy. It's the cooking of a place that was a royal capital for a thousand years and never forgot how to feed a crowd well. Let me walk you through what to eat.

## Start with the classics

- **Hwangnam-ppang (Gyeongju bread):** A thin, soft pastry packed with sweet red bean, stamped with a chrysanthemum. Born near Hwangnam-dong in 1939, it's still made by hand. Buy a warm one; eat it before it cools.
- **Ssambap:** The signature sit-down meal. Rice, grilled or braised meat, soybean paste, and a small forest of leaves and side dishes you wrap yourself. It's generous, balanced, and endlessly customisable.
- **Chalborira (barley bread) and beopju rice wine:** Older, quieter specialties. Beopju was once a court liquor — worth a small glass if you find it.

## Where the food comes from

Silla's capital drew nobles, monks, and merchants from across Asia, and the table had to match. That's why local cooking leans on abundance and ceremony — many dishes, shared slowly. The temple influence runs deep too: careful vegetables, restraint, respect for the ingredient.

> Don't rush a Gyeongju meal. The whole point is the unhurried spread — reach, wrap, talk, repeat.

## How I'd plan it

Ssambap for a proper lunch, Hwangnam-ppang as an afternoon treat, and one special dinner where the old court table is recreated with real care. If you're gathering a group or marking an occasion, that heritage style of dining — many hands, many dishes, one long table — is exactly how the Silla capital would have done it.
