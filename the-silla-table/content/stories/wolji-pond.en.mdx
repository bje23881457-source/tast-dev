Most visitors come to Wolji at night, when the pavilions glow gold on black water. It is beautiful — but the beauty is not an accident. It is engineering, and it is politics.

## A pond built to be endless

When King Munmu completed the pond in 674 CE, his gardeners shaped the shoreline so that from no single vantage point could you see the entire water surface. Stand anywhere, and the pond seems to continue around an unseen bend. For a Silla court that had just unified the peninsula, the message was quiet and deliberate: **this kingdom, like this water, has no visible end.**

## What the mud remembered

For centuries the pond was known as *Anapji* — "the pond of geese and ducks" — a name given long after Silla fell, when only wild birds remained. Then, in the 1970s, drainage revealed more than 30,000 artifacts in the silt: dice carved for royal drinking games, a perfectly preserved wooden boat, gilt-bronze scissors shaped like a bird.

> The court did not vanish. It sank, and waited to be read.

## Seeing it the Silla way

When I bring guests here after dark, I ask them not to photograph first. Walk the eastern path slowly. Notice how the largest pavilion never quite reveals the far shore. You are not looking at a view — you are standing inside a 1,300-year-old idea about power, patience, and things that do not end.
