Ask what Silla royalty ate and most guides will shrug. The court left us monuments, not menus. But if you read the drinking cups, the trade records, and the temples together, a table begins to set itself.

## A cuisine built on preservation

Before refrigeration, flavor was time. Silla kitchens mastered *jang* (fermented soy and bean pastes) and salted seafood that could survive the journey inland from the East Sea. What we now prize as delicacy — *ganjang gejang*, raw crab cured in soy — began as the practical genius of a court that wanted the sea at a mountain table.

## The floating cup

Among the artifacts pulled from Wolji Pond was a fourteen-sided wooden die inscribed with drinking-game commands: *empty a cup in one gulp*, *sing without moving*, *let others tease you and stay still*. Court dining was theatre. Wine cups floated down a winding stone channel, and if the cup reached you before you finished your poem, you drank.

> To eat like Silla is to understand that a meal was a performance of grace under pleasure.

## Tasting it today

At our table we do not reenact a museum. We cook the logic of that court — season, preservation, and play — into a meal you actually want to eat. The history is the seasoning, not the point.
